1
You are what you consume
We don't usually think about what we see, listen to, and generally experience as something that profoundly conditions our day. When choosing the right movie to watch or the next book to read, we may be selective and aware of our tastes. Still, when it comes to doomscrolling, checking the news, or going through that pile of emails, we often consume passively, letting content wash over us without much thought. We rely on algorithms that once made us discover that incredible recipe (which we still haven't tried) or a fascinating article about the "Top 35 museums in Europe" (we'll never visit). Yet, the nature of our consumption isn't just about the what but also the how and when.
Timing matters: Scrolling through social media first thing in the morning sets a tone for the day while doom-scrolling before bed can disrupt sleep.
The volume of consumption matters, too; being bombarded with an unending stream of information leaves little space for critical reflection or even genuine enjoyment.
Our brains aren't impermeable; they're porous sponges soaking up the signals we feed them. Every post, video, headline, or podcast carries a residue that lingers in our minds—sometimes in the form of anxiety, sometimes as an ephemeral dopamine hit, and occasionally as a meaningful insight. When we overconsume junk information, it can distort our perspective, amplify our biases, and erode our focus.
2
What even is a media diet?
If you think about it for a second, in this contemporary scenario, the concept of a media diet makes perfect sense: it compares good media consumption to a controlled food regime, emphasizing quality information over affirmation, avoiding overprocessed or sensationalized content, and seeking balance and diversity.
Briefly, a media diet is the deliberate and conscious consumption of information.
The goal is not necessarily or primarily to reduce information intake but to improve its quality and relevance, fostering better decision-making, reduced stress, and overall mental well-being.

Know your goals
All the diets begin with a weighting (literally but also in the broader sense) of the current status. So, it's essential to make a measure of the current status of media consumption: check your smartphone and your PC for app usage and overall screen time, and try to estimate the time spent on each media-related activity of your week: podcast, tv, newspaper, books, radio, email, chats, streaming, theatre...
Just making this, the awareness of something not so much in our control could already rise, but don't panic: you've alredy done the first step in a more ealthy direction and form here you can tart to reimagine your media consumption. Just keep in mind, that, as every diet, the results will not come quick: you have to make affordable steps and concentrate on maintainging improvements over time.

Some main elements of a good diet
We don't want to base our thoughts on a sense of guilt; instead, we should focus on the intent of a more genuine life. We have to be aware of the benefits we want to gain from this change. I've tried to summarize some of the principles I've based this reflection on, but I want to be clear that especially this part is a purely personal view of what I want for myself and in the time of my life: everyone is free to even strongly disagree with this view, as well as with the overall purpose of this little zine.
Less is less (and it's good)
Generally speaking, we tend to consume more than what we truly need and consume simpler and easier-to-process media. Most of this behaviour is due to the FAMO, which acts like a fridge full of delicacies right after the kitchen door: if we want to consume better, we have to consume less than what we would do and not be victims of our desires.
Avoid what puts distance between the real and the people (especially the ones around you)
